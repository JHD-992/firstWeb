Brief History of the Kruger

The park was first established in 1898 by the then President of the Transvaal, Paul Kruger. The park covers 19 633 square kilometers and is home to 753 species of animal and 1982 species of plants. The area is also rich in cultural heritage with 254 cultural heritage site including 130 rock art sites. The first game warden of the park James Stevenson-Hamilton was largely responsible for establishing the original park. This included the removal of local tribes from the area. These forced removals earned Stevenson-Hamilton the nickname "Skukuza" which means "He who sweeps clean" in the native Tsonga language. Areas of the appropriated land was given back to descendents of the native communties many years later and agreements reached so that they may now earn a share of the profits generated by the tourism in the area.

The modern day Kruger National Park forms part of the Great Limpopo Transfrontier Park which includes Gonarezhou National Park in Zimbabwe and Limpopo National Park in Mozambique. It has also undergone significant development to become a tourism hub with fully stocked stores, comfortable accomodation, recreation facilities and a new three-star hotel, The Skukuza Safari Lodge. Spread throughout the park are twelve rest camps where these amenties can be found as well as various picnic areas with varying resources
